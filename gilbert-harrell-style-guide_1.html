Gilbert Harrell Copywriting Style Guide

How we write for the web, with examples drawn from real GHSM copy.

The Voice in One Line

Measured, institutional, and quietly confident. We sound like a firm that has advised Georgia's business community since 1871 and has nothing to prove, because the record speaks for itself.

We lead with what the client gets, write as a present "we" and "our team," and let credentials substantiate rather than open. The voice runs in two registers: short and declarative in headlines and CTAs, fuller and more measured in the body. Formal but approachable, and never impersonal.

Core Principles

1. Lead with client outcomes; substantiate with specifics

Open on what the client gets or what the work accomplishes, not on the firm's trophy case. Credentials still matter, but they're how we back up a claim, not how we start. So: lead with the outcome, then let the American College of Trial Lawyers, the decades of experience, and the named practice areas do the proving. Never claim "excellence" in the abstract; show it.

2. The client is the subject; the firm is the guide

We help our clients achieve their goals. We advise, we navigate, we anticipate. The hero of the sentence is usually the client or the work, not us. That said, write as a visible "we" and "our team," never as an impersonal, faceless institution ("the firm provides..."). We're a present, named partner standing beside the client, not above them.

3. One image per section, then back to substance

A single, well-placed figure of speech ("tangled in the web of government regulation") earns its keep. A pile of them reads as filler. Allow yourself one flourish per section and make the rest of the prose clean and direct.

4. Continuity is part of who we are

Time, permanence, and long-term value are recurring themes for a reason. "Since 1871." "Decades of experience." "Long-term success." Lean into our longevity as a genuine differentiator, not a throwaway line.

5. Two registers: flowing body, declarative display

Body copy uses full, balanced sentences that build to a satisfying close, often with a slightly longer final clause. Read it aloud; if it stumbles, rewrite it. Headlines, subheads, and CTAs do the opposite: short, declarative, active. Think "CONFIDENTIAL. STRATEGIC. PROVEN." Clipped and confident up top, warm and measured in the paragraphs. Keep passive voice out of every heading and call to action.

6. Georgia and the region are part of the brand

Reference our Georgia and coastal/regional identity where it fits naturally. Longevity plus place ("a trusted partner to Georgia's business community since 1871") is a real differentiator. Don't force it, but don't strip it out either.

7. The firm does not editorialize about its own feelings

We state what we did and let the work speak. No "we're proud to," "we're glad to," "we're pleased to," or "we're excited to." Replace any feeling-statement with a plain statement of action: not "We're glad to have supported the Commission," but "Our firm has supported the Commission." Neutral and factual always wins.

What to Avoid

The fastest way to break this voice is to slip into generic marketing or AI-pattern writing. Watch for these specifically:

  • The "some do X, others do Y" setup. "Some milestones make headlines. Others quietly make the community safer." This is a tell. Cut it and state the thing directly.
  • Hollow intensifiers. "Quietly," "firmly," "truly," "every single day," "come to life." They add heat, not light.
  • Stock transformation imagery. "Where blueprints become a building." "Built for today, ready for tomorrow." Pleasant-sounding, says nothing.
  • Self-congratulation. Claiming excellence instead of demonstrating it.
  • Rule-of-three padding. Three parallel phrases where one precise one would do.

Some milestones make headlines. Others quietly make the community safer every single day. The new ARFF station now taking shape at BQK is firmly the second kind, and it's one we're proud to see come to life.

Construction is underway on a new Aircraft Rescue and Fire Fighting (ARFF) station at Brunswick Golden Isles Airport. For us, it's the latest in a long line of projects we've supported the Glynn County Airport Commission through.

That kind of forward-looking design is exactly what you want from a public safety investment: built for today, ready for tomorrow.

The station opens with three apparatus bays and is engineered to add a fourth, so it can expand as the airport's needs grow.

Off-voice

For a community, moments like that are a tangible sign of progress, the point where blueprints become a building.

On-voice

The topping-out ceremony, where officials marked the placement of the final structural beam, is a visible sign of how far the project has come.

Word Bank

Reach For

  • advise, counsel, represent
  • navigate, anticipate, position
  • practical, sophisticated, sustained
  • long-term value, decades, since 1871
  • trusted partner, client advocacy

Avoid

  • quietly, firmly, truly, simply
  • come to life, take shape, journey
  • every single day, game-changing
  • proud to / glad to / pleased to / excited to
  • cutting-edge, world-class, passionate

Pre-Publish Checklist

  • Does the piece open on a client outcome or the work, not the firm's credentials?
  • Did I claim anything I can't back with a specific fact or credential?
  • Is the client (or the work) the subject of most sentences, while still writing as a visible "we" / "our team"?
  • Are headlines and CTAs short, declarative, and active (no passive voice)?
  • Does the body copy read cleanly aloud, with full sentences and good rhythm?
  • Have I used more than one figure of speech in any single section?
  • Did I cut "quietly," "firmly," "truly," and "proud/excited to"?
  • Did I remove every firm feeling-statement ("we're glad/pleased/proud to") in favor of plain action?
  • Is there a "some do X, others do Y" construction hiding anywhere?
  • Does the longevity/continuity and Georgia/regional angle show up where it naturally fits?
